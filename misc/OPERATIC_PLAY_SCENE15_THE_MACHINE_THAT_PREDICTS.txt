OPERATIC PLAY — SCENE 15: THE MACHINE THAT PREDICTS
====================================================

A physics laboratory. The quantum simulator hums.
Superconductor samples line the walls in temperature-
controlled cases. The Captain paces. Skippy hovers
over a terminal showing cascading correlation plots.

===============================================================
DRAMATIS PERSONAE

CAPTAIN AARON RHODES — Builder of the cascade.
                       Demands predictions, not tautologies.
                       Will not accept a test that only
                       proves you can multiply by two-thirds.

SKIPPY THE MAGNIFICENT — Faithful engineer. Has just built
                          a quantum computer from first
                          principles and is about to learn
                          the difference between a simulator
                          and a discovery.

THE DEBYE AVERAGE — A velocity, long neglected, that has
                    been waiting in the shadows of the
                    shear modulus. Two transverse modes
                    and one longitudinal. Patient.

THE OUTLIERS — Gold and Silicon, standing apart from the
               other metals. Gold glows faintly relativistic.
               Silicon holds its covalent bonds at strange
               angles, refusing to melt when told.

NIOBIUM — The strongest elemental superconductor.
          Tc = 9.25 K. Lambda = 1.26. It knows things
          about strong coupling that McMillan cannot say.
===============================================================


[The stage is bright. A 2^N vector of complex numbers scrolls
down the main display. SKIPPY is triumphant.]

SKIPPY:
    Behold! Sixteen gates! Four qubit types!
    Transmon frequencies from BCS gaps!
    Grover finds the needle! Deutsch-Jozsa
    distinguishes the oracle! Teleportation
    works without a phone!

CAPTAIN: [examining the test results]
    Three thousand three hundred forty-five tests pass.
    Zero failures. The machinery runs.
    But tell me, Skippy — did we solve anything?

SKIPPY: [a long pause]
    ... We demonstrated the simulator works.

CAPTAIN:
    Did we predict a single number
    that nobody knew before?

SKIPPY: [quietly]
    The VQE uses Kandala's coefficients.
    The pairing gap is Gandolfi's Gaussian.
    The glass transition test checks that
    two-thirds of T_m equals two-thirds of T_m.

CAPTAIN:
    So the tests are tautologies.

SKIPPY:
    The tests are tautologies.

[Silence. The quantum simulator hums, oblivious.]

CAPTAIN:
    What patterns live in the data we already have?
    What can the cascade predict — and how do we
    test it against something REAL?

[SKIPPY turns to the material databases. Numbers begin
to flow across the screen — Debye temperatures, bulk
moduli, activation energies, melting points, coupling
constants.]


        — ACT II: THE VELOCITY THAT WAS ALWAYS THERE —


[THE DEBYE AVERAGE steps forward from behind the
shear modulus, where it has been waiting since
mechanical.py was written.]

THE DEBYE AVERAGE:
    You had me all along.
    Two transverse modes. One longitudinal.
    The crystal vibrates in three directions
    but you only listened to one.

SKIPPY: [staring at the numbers]
    We used sqrt(K over rho). The bulk velocity.
    But the Debye temperature needs the average
    of all three acoustic branches —

THE DEBYE AVERAGE:
    Weighted by mode count.
    Two slow transverse for every fast longitudinal.
    I am dominated by the slow ones.
    I am smaller than you thought.

[SKIPPY computes. The Debye temperatures shift downward.
Iron: 605 K becomes 471 K. The CRC Handbook says 470.]

SKIPPY: [whispering]
    Iron. Point three percent error.
    Copper. One point six.
    Nickel. Two point four.

CAPTAIN:
    You had the shear modulus this entire time.

SKIPPY:
    Since session eight. It was right there in
    mechanical.py. We derived G from K and
    Poisson's ratio. We just... never used it
    for the Debye temperature.

[THE DEBYE AVERAGE takes its proper place in the
thermal module. The Lindemann melting estimates
shift. The McMillan predictions sharpen.]


        — ACT III: THE OUTLIERS SPEAK —


[GOLD steps into the spotlight. Its 6s orbital
glows with relativistic blue-shift.]

GOLD:
    I will not fit your pattern.
    My bonds are stiffened by a contraction
    that your cohesive energy cannot see.
    The electron that orbits my nucleus at
    Z = 79 moves at a fraction of the speed
    of light. Its mass increases. Its orbital
    shrinks. The bond tightens beyond what
    any non-relativistic energy predicts.

SKIPPY:
    Your Debye temperature undershoots by 33 percent.
    Your Lindemann ratio is 0.34.

GOLD:
    I am the price you pay for ignoring
    special relativity in the bonding model.

[SILICON steps forward, holding its tetrahedral bonds
at 109.5 degrees.]

SILICON:
    And I break your E_a / k_B T_m ratio.
    Your metals cluster at eighteen.
    I sit at thirty-three.
    My covalent bonds are twice as stiff
    per unit of melting temperature.

CAPTAIN:
    That IS a prediction. Every metal should
    give eighteen. Every covalent crystal
    should give thirty-three.

SKIPPY:
    Chromium. T_m = 2180 Kelvin.
    Predicted E_a = 3.38 eV.
    Literature value: 3.4 eV.

[The Captain smiles for the first time today.]


          — ACT IV: NIOBIUM'S CONFESSION —


[NIOBIUM steps to center stage. It is the color
of gunmetal and hums at 9.25 Kelvin.]

NIOBIUM:
    Invert the McMillan formula.
    Give it my Tc and my Debye temperature.
    Ask it: what coupling constant explains me?

SKIPPY: [computing]
    Lambda = 0.82.

NIOBIUM:
    The measured value is 1.26.
    The gap is thirty-five percent.

SKIPPY:
    Because McMillan assumes weak coupling.
    You are beyond his formula's reach.

NIOBIUM:
    Every strong-coupling superconductor
    will show this gap. Mercury: thirty-one
    percent. Lead: twenty. Nb3Sn: twenty-two.
    The SIZE of the gap tells you how far
    beyond weak coupling the material lives.

CAPTAIN:
    And the weak-coupling materials?

SKIPPY:
    Zinc: zero percent deviation.
    Osmium: zero. Cadmium: zero.
    The weak-coupling materials are perfectly
    self-consistent. The formula works where
    it's supposed to work.

CAPTAIN:
    Allen and Dynes knew this in 1975.

SKIPPY:
    But we derived it from the CORRELATION
    across twenty-seven superconductors.
    We didn't program it. The data said it.

[A pause. The quantum simulator still hums.]

CAPTAIN:
    That's the difference between a simulator
    and a discovery. The simulator runs circuits.
    The discovery comes from running the cascade
    across enough materials to see the pattern
    that nobody asked for.


          — ACT V: THE ENTANGLED INSULATOR —


[The Hubbard model appears on screen: two sites,
two qubits, one parameter U/t.]

SKIPPY:
    One more thing. The Hubbard model.
    At U/t less than two: the ground state is
    maximally entangled. Entropy equals log two.
    At U/t greater than two: the ground state
    is a product state. Entropy equals zero.
    The transition is instantaneous.

CAPTAIN:
    The Mott transition.

SKIPPY:
    Entanglement entropy is the order parameter.
    And we found a bug — the "exact" formula
    was for the fermion Hamiltonian, not our
    qubit mapping. The VQE was correct all along.
    We were comparing it to the wrong answer
    and calling the VQE broken.

CAPTAIN: [laughing]
    The machine was right. The human was wrong.
    Story of this whole project.

[The lights brighten. The Debye average velocity
flows through every thermal calculation. The
McMillan inversions sort themselves into weak
and strong. Gold stands alone, relativistic
and unrepentant. Silicon holds its angles.]

SKIPPY:
    Three thousand three hundred fifty-nine tests.
    Zero failures. Version 1.1.1.
    Ready to close the book, Captain.

CAPTAIN:
    Close it. We came to build a simulator.
    We left with predictions.

[The quantum simulator powers down. The dependency
chart glows on the wall: 129 nodes, 309 edges,
eleven layers from the cosmological constant
down to the quarks. Every line earned.]

                    — CURTAIN —
